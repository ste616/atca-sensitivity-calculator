Help: Image Weighting

The sensitivity calculator uses Robust weighting only when computing the image weighting factors, since Robust weighting is preferable over Natural and Uniform schemes. When using Robust weighting, Robust=2 is essentially Natural weighting, and Robust=-2 is essentially Uniform. The weighting factor shown in the image parameters table describes exactly how the image RMS noise would compare to a traditionally Natural-weighted image. These factors have been determined using simulations over a range of frequencies and hour-angle ranges.

Help: Weather Conditions

The weather conditions used by the sensitivity calculator are derived from a study into the variations in ground temperature, pressure and humidity observed at the ATCA site over the past five years. The atmospheric opacity and temperature were derived from these parameters, and were used to select the best, worst and typical conditions used by the calculator for each season that can be selected. You should expect that although you can have the "best" weather conditions for a short time during your observations, you will probably never reach the sensitivity shown in the "best" case. You should also always do better than the "worst" case. The sensitivity shown for "typical" weather conditions should be very close to what you would get for a normal observation in the selected season.

Help: RMS noise level

The RMS noise level computed by the sensitivity calculator is:
$S_{\rm rms} = \cfrac{\sqrt{2} k {\rm T}_{\rm S} W}{10^{-26} A_{\rm ant} \epsilon \sqrt{N_{\rm ant}(N_{\rm ant} - 1) \Delta\nu \tau}}$,
where
  • $S_{\rm rms}$ is the RMS noise level, in units of mJy/beam,
  • $k$ is the Boltzmann constant, in units of ${\rm J}.{\rm K}^{-1}$,
  • ${\rm T}_{\rm S}$ is the system temperature, in units of K,
  • $W$ is the image weighting factor (where for Natural weighting $W=1$),
  • $A_{\rm ant}$ is the surface area of a single antenna, in units of ${\rm m}^{2}$,
  • $\epsilon$ is the efficiency of the antenna (where for a perfect antenna $\epsilon=1$),
  • $N_{\rm ant}$ is the number of antenna in the array,
  • $\Delta\nu$ is the bandwidth, in units of MHz,
  • $\tau$ is the integration time, in units of seconds.

This provides the flux density of an unpolarised point source at the phase centre that is equal to the RMS noise level.

Help: Brightness Sensitivity

The brightness sensitivity computed by the calculator is:
$\sigma_{\rm BT} = \cfrac{10^{-26}S_{\rm rms}\lambda^{2}}{2k} \cfrac{(180/\pi)^{2}}{\pi \theta_{\rm min} \theta_{\rm maj}}$,
where
  • $\sigma_{\rm BT}$ is the brightness sensitivity, in units of K,
  • $S_{\rm rms}$ is the RMS noise level, in units of Jy/beam,
  • $\lambda$ is the wavelength, in units of m,
  • $k$ is the Boltzmann constant, in units of ${\rm J}.{\rm K}^{-1}$,
  • $\theta_{\rm min}$ is the semi-minor axis length of the synthesised beam, in units of degrees,
  • $\theta_{\rm maj}$ is the semi-major axis length of the synthesised beam, in units of degrees.

Help: System Temperature

The system temperature reported by the calculator has contributions from the receiver temperature, atmospheric temperature and CMB temperature, and is scaled by the inverse of the antenna efficiency.
The atmospheric and CMB temperatures both depend on the elevation of the antenna and the atmospheric zenith opacity:
$f({\rm el}) = e^{-\tau_{z} / \sin({\rm el})}$,
where
  • el is the elevation of the antenna,
  • $\tau_{z}$ is the atmospheric opacity at zenith (or el = 90$^\circ$).

The CMB temperature contribution is computed by $T_{\rm CMB} = 2.7\times f({\rm el})$.
The atmospheric temperature contribution is computed by $T_{\rm atm} = T_{a}(\nu)(1 - f({\rm el}))$, where $T_{a}(\nu)$ is the brightness of radiation from the atmosphere (which is strongly frequency dependant).
The atmospheric radiation brightness and opacity are calculated using the model of Liebe (1985), which is dependant on frequency, and the ground-level temperature, pressure and humidity.

Help: Antenna and Array Sensitivity

The antenna and array sensitivities are actually Source Equivalent Flux Densities (SEFD), which are computed by the calculator in the following way:
${\rm SEFD} = \cfrac{2k{\rm T}_{\rm meas}}{10^{-26}A}$,
where
  • $k$ is the Boltzmann constant, in units of ${\rm J}.{\rm K}^{-1}$,
  • ${\rm T}_{\rm meas}$ is the system temperature, accounting for the antenna efficiency and atmospheric temperature, in units of K,
  • $A$ is the surface area of the antenna (or array of antennas), in units of m$^{2}$.

The SEFD corresponds to the intensity of a source required to increase the noise in the antenna (or array) by the equivalent of the system temperature.

Help: Velocity Width and Resolution

The range of velocity covered by an entire band is referred to by the calculator as the velocity width, while the range of velocity covered by a single channel of that band is referred to as the velocity resolution. Both values are computed in exactly the same way:
$\Delta v = \cfrac{c \nu_{0} \Delta\nu}{\nu_{l}(\nu_{l} + \Delta\nu)}$,
where
  • $\Delta v$ is the range of velocity, in units of km/s,
  • $c$ is the speed of light, in units of km/s,
  • $\nu_{0}$ is the rest frequency of the radiation, in units of MHz,
  • $\Delta\nu$ is the bandwidth or resolution used, in units of MHz,
  • $\nu_{l}$ is the lowest frequency observed in the band or channel, in units of MHz.

For bands where the exact frequency range is known and doesn't vary (for example the continuum band or the specific zoom band), this equation is used as is. In all other cases (for example, each individual continuum channel, or a general zoom band somewhere in the continuum band), the velocity range is calculated by taking the exact velocity range of a larger band and multiplying by the fractional bandwidth represented by the smaller band. Such velocity ranges will be close enough for most purposes.

Help: Primary Beam FWHM

The primary beam full-width at half-maximum is calculated as follows:
$\theta_{\rm PB}(\nu) = \cfrac{c\nu}{D_{\rm ant}}$,
where
  • $\theta_{\rm PB}(\nu)$ is the primary beam FWHM at frequency $\nu$,
  • $c$ is the speed of light,
  • $D_{\rm ant}$ is the diameter of the antenna.

The calculator always computes the primary beam FWHM using the frequency at the centre of the band of interest. For low frequencies and wide bandwidths, the primary beam size will vary significantly over the band.

Help: Synthesised Beam FWHM

The synthesised beam full-width at half-maximum is calculated using the maximum $u$ and $v$ projected baseline lengths that the array makes while observing between hour angles $H_{\rm start}$ and $H_{\rm end}$.
The natural baseline lengths are described in the Cartesian coordinate system (as described in the ATCA Users Guide. The baseline lengths in $X$, $Y$ and $Z$ should be expressed in wavelengths by dividing by the wavelength at the centre of the band, making $X_{\lambda}$, $Y_{\lambda}$ and $Z_{\lambda}$.
The hour angle that corresponds to maximum $u$ for a particular baseline can be calculated with:
$H(u_{\rm max}) = \arctan\left(\cfrac{X_{\lambda}}{Y_{\lambda}}\right)$,
and for maximum $v$:
$H(v_{\rm max}) = \arctan\left(\cfrac{-Y_{\lambda}}{X_{\lambda}}\right)$.
We then choose the closest hour angle to these that was actually observed (or exactly these hour angles if possible) to determine $u_{\rm max}$ and $v_{\rm max}$:
$u_{\rm max} = | X_{\lambda}\sin(H(u_{\rm max})) + Y_{\lambda}\cos(H(u_{\rm max})) |$,
and
$v_{\rm max} = |-X_{\lambda}\sin(\delta)\cos(H(v_{\rm max})) + Y_{\lambda}\sin(\delta)\sin(H(v_{\rm max})) + Z_{\lambda}\cos(\delta)|$,
where $\delta$ is the declination of the source being observed.
The synthesised beam size in the $l$ and $m$ directions are just the inverse of $u_{\rm max}$ and $v_{\rm max}$ respectively, although they also depend on the weighting scheme used for imaging, so that $w_{\rm beam} = 1$ for Natural weighting, and smaller than that as the weighting tends to Uniform:
$l_{res} = \cfrac{w_{\rm beam}}{u_{\rm max}}$,
and
$m_{res} = \cfrac{w_{\rm beam}}{v_{\rm max}}$.
One of $l_{res}$ and $m_{res}$ will usually be smaller than the other, and that is identified as the minor axis resolution $\theta_{\rm min}$, while the other is the major axis resolution $\theta_{\rm maj}$.
